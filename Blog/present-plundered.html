Present Plundered

Thu • 17 Jul 2025

Work for money or 'job' is one of the most important aspects of every human being's life. People devote a huge portion of their lives to work. Often times, it is the focal point of every political agenda of political parties or at least the ostentation to garner support. It is an irrefutable truth that work has significantly changed the way we live our lives. All the food, clothing, buildings and gadgets that surround us are the outcome of work. However, nothing in this universe is absolute or devoid of limitations. Thus, we must question the purpose of work, especially in the context of the contemporary world, where work is scarce and pay is less.

Before proceeding further, we must be absolutely clear about what is meant by job. It means to come to an agreement with an employer to perform a set of tasks in return for monetary benefits. In other words, it is the sale of one's labour energy and time for money.

Now why do people work? The primary reason behind work is to solve the problem of scarcity. Nature has no doubt endowed all organisms with resources to develop themselves. But, in order to really make use of the resources, all organisms must work to some degree. In the hunter and gatherer period, animals and fruits were abundant, but they would not simply walk up to people's plates. The final product -- food on the plate -- was scarce and hence, humans 'worked' in the form of hunting animals and gathering fruits from trees. After satisfying their wants, they enjoyed their leisure time.

The situation is a bit complicated in today's age. Technology has empowered us to achieve remarkable feats. One particular feat is increase in productivity. It has enabled us to produce huge amounts of goods in less time and with less capital. The consequence is that we no longer live in a world of scarcity but overabundance. A moment's contemplation makes this apparent. Whenever we want something, we visit a store, find it and buy it. E-commerce has made it even more convenient. We can buy anything from the comfort of our homes within a short span of time. The core message is this: whatever we need, whenever we need has already been produced. In fact, so many goods are produced today that a large proportion of the goods end up in the landfills as nobody needs them. If we dig deeper, we also realise that only a tiny minority of the population is doing something that improves the material conditions for the rest of the members of the society. The majority are engaged in unnecessary bureaucracy, management, speculation or roles that exist to make others appear noble. Sadly, the first three are the ones who are rewarded the most. This is surely not sustainable for the environment and furthermore it adversely impacts individuals and society. Moreover, the very fact that there is a scarcity of jobs or increase in unemployment confirms that we no longer need to work in order to satisfy needs.

The premise of work makes the reality questionable. In spite of achieving overabundance, we still insist on maintaining full employment. Abundance stimulated by technology should have been joyous news. We should have had more leisure time to enjoy however we wanted. Yet, the very development stands before us as an obstacle. The advent of automation and AI poses a threat to humanity, not in the sense predicted in sci-fi fiction (robots enslaving humans), but by liberating humans from work.

So if the problem of scarcity is solved, what would happen if we sit back a little and enjoy the wealth generated? What if we stop work once the general consumption level of output is attained, distribute the produce using a rational system and work minimally to maintain that level? Conventional wisdom dictates that it would be disastrous. After all it is said, 'an idle brain is the devil's workshop'. Living an idle (not working for money) and frivolous life is evil, or at least it is a privilege reserved for the elites. Moreover, the phantom by the name 'economy' would collapse due to stagnation in GDP. Nevertheless, we believe a more convincing and healthy outcome may be expressed by the phrase 'empty mind is a beautiful mind'. First, it would clearly give the environment a relief. Work consumes a lot of energy. Workers travel every morning from their home to their workplace. In a motor-centric country such as India, workers have no other choice than to use motor vehicles. At work, various machineries consume energy; Lights, fans, and heavy machines which are needed to transform raw materials into finished products. In a nutshell, a lot of energy would be conserved. Less materials would be extracted from the depth of the Earth. Less paper would be used which has plagued this age of intense bureaucracy. Pollution level will also plummet significantly. Last but not least, the superfluous products produced would not be discarded after collecting cobwebs on the shelves.

Second, people would have a lot of free time to actually do something meaningful. When people toil for ten to twelve hours every day which consumes a lot of their time and vitality, they become indifferent zombies, incapable of thinking how the elites of society are controlling them. Even if they do manage to deduce this, they remain compliant and rationalise their inaction. With leisure, people will have the power to educate themselves on various socio-political matters and actually hold the authorities responsible. Presently, this privilege is available to the elites and a tiny portion of working class who are better off than the majority. People will be obligated to question on important issues, such as inflation, deteriorating roads, collapsing bridges and unhygienic water. Democracy will finally strengthen. Elites keep insisting the importance of hard work and that work is 'essential' for individuals in order to subjugate people, thereby leading lavish lives themselves. They even promote their distorted 'rags to riches' stories to prove this.

Finally, this would positively impact society as a whole. As mentioned earlier, modern work depletes life out of people. When their mind become numb after constant toiling, they do not have the energy to think about anything. They will accept anything that is presented before them. All the institutions, in the present society, whether explicit or implicit, reinforce the idea of consumerism. People are brainwashed into believing that their sole purpose in life is to amass money and products. The general population admires people like Adani and Ambani who exploit the masses and hoard vast amount of wealth. Media, education system in alliance with the state instills these exploitative, individualistic traits in the minds of people. Advertisements make addictive advertisements and persuade people to buy their products. The numb consumers, incapable of understanding anything, believe that buying the products would make them 'successful'. A careful observer would clearly see that most advertisements do not sell the product by highlighting its utility, but rather sell the idea that buying the product would enhance one's status. The products make them competitors of other obedient consumers. This is the perfection of slavery. Everyone are in constant competition with one another for projecting their superior status. As a consequence, people hate one another and are incapable of cooperating with one another. "The greatest trick the Devil ever pulled was convincing the world he didn’t exist" is the appropriate quote to summarise this. One must be endowed with huge capacity of self-delusion to even remotely believe that such society will ever become 'Vishwaguru'. Once people reclaim their minds by refraining the torture, they would finally have a chance to realise that individualism and consumerism robbed them of their own liberty. They will finally have a chance to imagine what a better society would look like.

Perhaps the elites are well aware of the power of the masses and thus preach the importance of career or having a job, and ensure that the mass remain sterile, and believe that this is the only rational way of behaving. The so-called 'pragmatic' individuals or the 'bootlickers of the elites' might dismiss this proposition by saying that it is very utopian and in contradiction of some abstract idea as 'human nature'. These people are the ones who lack imagination and understanding of history the most. They see injustice and corruption everywhere and instead of facing them, they condone them. They further project their own limitations onto others. Under the present socio-economic system, only a tiny minority owns the means of production; this means the resources and assets that stir production, say offices and factories. The large sections of our society owns nothing except for their own labour. This makes it imperative for them to work or sell their labour power and time for money in order to purchase the very products that they have worked hard on to bring into reality and sustain themselves.

With increased productivity, workers easily produce enough products as required by society, but as already explained, they do not own the means of production and have no right upon the final products. They are only paid in money to go to the workplace and produce more. The money rewarded is barely enough to sustain a few days, and hence they must go back to work again and again. The result is the superfluous products that no one wants.

As productivity increases, the use of resources increase and not decrease. This counterintuitive idea is called 'Jevons paradox'. One who has even a little understanding of the current socio-economic system would easily see the reason. The profit that is accumulated due to increased productivity is re-invested with the hope of even more profit. Thus, even more resources end up being used to satisfy the investors. Workers is also one resource that is not spared. But, no new jobs are created because it is easy to overwork the existing workers without any additional benefits by instigating fear of unemployment than to hire new people and pay them. This cycle continues on and on. By the way, in biology, this incessant growth behaviour is called 'cancer'.

A society where work is done to improve and maintain material conditions without much ecological, psychological and societal damage is possible. Mainstream economic theories and methods are ineffective for solving the issue. They have been devised with the sole purpose of destroying everything except the hegemony of the elites. Realisation that the current system is absurd is the first step towards change. No change can come about until and unless we first accept that there is a problem. It would also be naive of us to believe that simply demonising modern work and abandoning it is the solution. It is a necessary solution but not sufficient. As already discussed, people have internalised the logic of the system and thus are hesitant of bringing change. They believe that money is the absolute reality, accumulating more money is the only purpose in life. That society only breeds corruption, jealousy and greed. The damage done by the system would certainly require 'work' to restore.
