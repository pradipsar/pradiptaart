Taxation Myth

Wed • 4 Feb 2026

There is a big misconception regarding the role of taxes in our life. We are often taught to believe that taxes are paid to the government to fund social welfare. Officials frequently impose high taxes without delivering proportionate benefits. They justify their actions in the name of 'saving the economy' or 'government does not have money' or simply blame the previous government. They use technical jargon like GDP, fiscal deficit, debt and so on to hide their incompetency, if not lying. However, some serious scrutiny reveals their claims to be based on obsolete ideas which no longer are applicable.

To understand the futility of levying high taxes in order to achieve their goals, one must first understand why exactly tax is levied and how our socio-economic system functions.

Reserve Bank of India (RBI) was established in 1935 as the central bank of India. As the central bank, it was assigned to regulate the issue of currency and maintain reserves in order to attain monetary stability. The government has the sole monopoly on currency.

In 1956, it adopted Minimum Reserve System. Under the system, the RBI is required to maintain a minimum reserve of ₹200 crore. Fulfilling this criterion renders the RBI power to print infinite amount of money. With the advent of technology, the RBI doesn't even need to print money. It simply has to credit (increase bank account value) of the beneficiary and thus money is created, just from thin air. Both the actions, whether printing money or crediting account make no difference as ultimately, both increase the liability of the government. Money is not a physical reality, but an abstract construct. RBI does not interact with the public directly, but indirectly through commercial banks. Banks are legally accountable to the central bank and must comply with the norms. The corollary of Minimum Reserve System is that all social projects can be funded: funding educational institutions, providing quality healthcare at subsidised rate or free of cost, etc. Government is not constrained by the revenue it generates by imposing taxes on citizens. Government can never run out of money, at least until it preserves its sovereignty.

Some would argue that creating a lot of money would naturally lead to inflation, i.e fall in the value of currency. What used to be ₹10 would now require more money. Their conclusion is based on a tacit assumption, that society has achieved maximum production capacity; there are no resources lying idle and full employment has been achieved, which is obviously not the case.

It is empirically proven that money creation doesn't automatically lead to inflation. Since 1980, Japan's public debt has increased to reach 239% of GDP (a very big debt) from around 50%. Meanwhile, the inflation has remained consistently low and even turned negative for a few years. Similarly, in the case of Weimer Republic, hyperinflation in the 1920s, experts recount that hyperinflation was the cause of excessive currency printing, not the effect. Mainly, the disaster was driven by the brutal reparation cost imposed by the USA and its allies, the cost that Germany was simply incapable of paying, and the fact that the winners of war knew very well.

We are indoctrinated to believe that taxes fund all social projects. As if government is essentially bankrupt & it needs our money to do something. However, it has already been explained that government has the power to create infinite amount of money & cannot actually run out of money. It has the sole monopoly authority in the matter of money. Moreover, creating a lot of money does not always lead to inflation. We would explore this more later. Our whole understanding of the matter is backwards. We believe that government first imposes tax and then spends; however, in reality it first spends and then imposes tax.

So, what are taxes really for? The simple answer is to create demand for money. Imagine a house in which 10 people are living. One of them tells others that if they do his chores, he would give them a piece of paper with his face on it. Others would probably scoff at him and reject his demands. However, if he gains some form of authority over others—maybe he owns the house or hires a thug to discipline others—he can make a rule that every month, each member must give him that paper in order to live in the house, and the only way they could get the paper is to work for him. In such a situation, they have no other choice than to obey. Eventually, the members would also use it amongst themselves since they all need the paper to honour their obligations.

This is the major reason for tax. Tax is imposed to give value to unit of account (rupees in our case) or in other words, make rupees the medium of exchange. This is also the reason why rupees is not used as a medium of exchange in foreign countries; their government would not accept it to settle tax liability, fines and other debt obligations. Tax is simply not imposed to pay for social welfare.

There is another function of tax which is the outcome of the first one. This is to command and mobilise resources. When the government imposes a ₹1 crore tax, individuals must sell goods or services worth at least ₹1 crore to the government to discharge their obligations. Government would then necessarily have to create at least ₹1 crore. However, people also want to save money for future and present needs. For this reason, government must spend more than just ₹1 crore. It would spend money up to the requirement of people to pay taxes and save money for future use. The amount spent will depend on the public’s desire to save, but it will remain below full productive capacity—since people also need goods and services for personal consumption. The deficit or public debt is therefore the savings with the public.

Another important function of tax is regulating inflation. If the government spends heavily without corresponding taxation, the private sector would have a lot of money at their disposal. Eventually, the government would have to compete with the private sector for limited resources. Each of them would have to offer a higher price to attract the resources, leading to inflation. If the government levies effective taxes, the private sector would be stripped from the power to offer higher prices and inflation can easily be mitigated.

Inflation generally occurs when no resources are idle but demand continues to rise. This is easy to see: scarcity empowers sellers, who can raise prices, leaving buyers with little choice but to accept them. Inflation can also arise before full employment of resources. For instance, if the government spends heavily on a single industry, that industry may eventually saturate, reaching a point where further growth is impossible—similar to the scenario of full resource utilisation. At that stage, the industry will drive up prices for others. Similarly, funding non-productive policies—such as direct cash transfers without corresponding increase in productive capacity—would lead to inflation. In such cases, spending does not expand the capacity of the society to produce goods and services, leaving sellers in power to increase prices as demand outpaces supply. Government spending, targeted towards increasing productive capacity and alleviating unemployment is not inflationary, provided it is matched with corresponding tax liabilities to prevent excess demand. This perspective contrasts sharply with mainstream ideology, which assumes that the government must first collects taxes before spending. In this view, spending is constrained by tax revenue.

This clearly illustrates that the value of money is not determined solely by quantity of money, but by government policy and the availability of resources. There is no 'Scientific law' that dictates money creation always leads to inflation.

With liberalisation in 1991, India opened its so called 'economy' to foreign capital, integrating itself into the global capitalist framework. Neoliberals hope for the utopia in which private sector dominates the society. They demonise government's existence and intervention, until a private company collapses and then they plead the government to intervene. Yes, hypocrisy and self-conflict are essential characteristics of this system. In this attempt, our government has taken crucial steps: selling the national wealth to the private sector and actively promoting their role. The sole logic and objective of this system is to create profit for the sake of profit. Production is no longer driven by necessity, but by expectations about future profits. This behaviour is even normalised, as if we have learnt nothing from biology—cells growing indefinitely (cancer) kill the entire host. Overall, we have a system in which the entire society, become slave of the capital. Government too serves the capital, not the people.

With this basic understanding of the system, we can further understand why the society is on the verge of collapse. Today, we observe that most industries are dominated by a tiny number of private companies, generally fewer than ten. So, this oligarchy is empowered to set as much price as it wishes. Companies are driven by shareholders demanding more and more profits every year. Raising price is one solution. The other part of the equation is cost. They would want to suppress cost as much as possible. This involves paying as little wages to workers. As previously explained, the government can buy any amount of goods and services that society is willing to sell, making its price the effective minimum wage. The private sector must offer more in order to compete with the government. However, with the government subordinated to the system, it fails to exercise its power, leading to mass unemployment. Unemployment reduces workers' bargaining power, further reducing wages. Thus, while prices of goods and services rise, wages stagnate or fall. To say that unemployment is a powerful tool in a capitalist society in order to control the mass and profit is not at all an exaggeration. Unlike most of what we believe, value of money, inflation, employment do not lie in the purview of 'economic sphere' but are largely 'political agenda'.

Throughout our analysis, we have ignored foreign trade. Yes, high net import can be a serious issue, especially for a country like India. India mainly relies on dollars for foreign trade. As net import increase and the value of rupee fall in relation to dollar, India's obligation to the world would increase significantly. For every dollar import, India would have to export more resources, depleting the country of its own wealth. It is no longer available for regional development. However, domestic debt is not similar to foreign debt. No doubt when considered as a unit in the world economy, India cannot exert much dominance and is subject to international politics. But, domestic debt is owed to the people within the jurisdiction of the nation, where it has sole monopoly on its currency. in that context, it is not constrained by revenue it generates from people and can spend more without worrying about economic turmoil. It can spend to nurture the productive capacity of the society in the light of sociological and ecological limits. It can ensure full employment, provide quality educational, healthcare, transportation services, in short undertake all social projects without using economic logic. The only reason it decides to not do it is to content the ruling interests.

At present, the officials at power preach about the taxation myth. They posit that government functions similar to a business enterprise or household, and they must balance their expenses and revenue. They even believe that government surplus implies wellness of general public. There seems to be no limits to their delusions. However, these very delusions stand as real doom for the mass, especially the so called 'middle class'. We have already mentioned that public debt is actually saving with the public. As on March 2025, public debt stands around ₹175 thousand crore. A large portion of the debt accrues to the members of the ruling class. They either put this money in the stock market or waste on frivolities. The money not only fails to improve the living conditions of the mass in any way, but will never circulate in the society. Thus, the leakage renders the deficit irrecoverable. Yet, authorities are adamant on closing the deficit with taxes. The class disposition of the government enables the elites to easily and legally evade lax. Also, as the tax liability on marginalised sections of the society is negligible, the whole burden to "balance" the budget lies on the middle class individuals. People believe that their taxes pay the government workers, when in reality it pays the ultra rich.

Even using their logic, that increasing government deficit (expenditure - revenue or government spending - taxes) would lead to economic turmoil, we face another problem. Higher taxes strip individuals from their excess purchasing power. They would have used it to consume something if not for the tax. Increase in taxes imply decrease in consumption. Therefore, products lying in stores wouldn't be sold, forcing producers to close down factories & laying off workers. The spiral of reduced purchasing power exacerbates the turmoil. Now, the government doesn't even have a tax base to impose tax on!

In conclusion, tax is a tool used by authority to mobilise resources through the society, give value to the unit of account and control inflation. The government has infinite money and does not depend on taxes to undertake social welfare schemes. Imposition of high taxes, with the intention of balancing the deficit is futile, but can be useful if imposed to achieve other social goals. For instance, a high tax on wealth targeted effectively towards the elites. The tax is not to fund government spending, but to reduce income inequality in the society, which enables the elites to influence the government in the first place.

They know it, yet they do it, by reducing the reality to the abstraction, propaganda.
