My Opinion on Reality

Mon • 16 Feb 2026

Materialism is the theory that physical matter is the only reality and that everything, including thought, feeling, and mind can be explained in terms of matter.

Our experience is determined by the configurations of our biological faculties: all experience takes place in space and time, is governed by causation, and possesses qualities such as colour. This perspective is common to both materialism and Transcendental Idealism. To say our experience is shaped by biological faculties does not contradict materialism or transcendental Idealism.

The key differentiating factor is this: if our experience must conform to the capabilities of our apparatus, then to claim that matter is the only reality (the absolute one) is at best speculation, or a fervent belief reinforced by the humanity's self-obsession. The old mistake of thinking too highly of oneself, born of ignorance. It is more probable that if an absolute reality exists (one we have no knowledge of and never will), it is formless and under no compulsion to have structures, unlike our experience. This is a crucial distinction between materialism and transcendental idealism.

There are numerous simple organisms whose eyes can only detect light. Our eyes add another dimension: colour. There is every reason to believe that our eyes lack at least one more dimension that would grant access to the absolute reality. Science has developed numerous instruments, such as the infrared camera. Perhaps that might alter our whole reality. This is not to say the missing dimension is one that science has revealed, because it might be one beyond our comprehension entirely.

According to materialism, matter lies outside ourselves, but in reality it is at best a creation of our mind. No one has seen atoms, we have merely deduced their existence using clever methods. We observe that everything is governed by causation, space, and time: I hit a wall and feel pain, but the wall does not flinch. All of this experience, to the best of our knowledge, arises from our perceptive capabilities. From this, our minds construct the idea of atoms: the microscopic solar system. The creation is noble and has enabled us to accomplish numerous feats, ranging from medicine to technology. Nonetheless, atom is not an absolute reality independent of our perception and consciousness, but rather its creation.

If we wish to understand our thoughts, feelings, and everything around us in terms of atoms, we are essentially trying to understand our experience using an idea created from that very experience. Our perception and consciousness created the concept of the atom—or rather, atom is understood through our experience—and now we seek to understand that same perception and consciousness in terms of atom. This appears to be a futile endeavour. Suppose we encounter something we found delicious when we ate it. We observe similar objects and notice the same colour. So far, we have relied on our experiences through mouth and eyes. Because our memory is reliable and we have learned to distinguish sounds with our ears, we assign it a particular sound: "apple." The symbol "apple" is an abstraction we created for convenience, to communicate with others about it. Yet we come to believe that the sound represents the original experience of seeing, tasting, and smelling. The abstraction we created has replaced the actual experience. While the word evokes an experience similar to actually seeing and tasting, they are fundamentally different. We created the abstraction of the atom and now mistake it for the experience itself.

The very idea that we possess a sensory system that creates sensory experience is itself a sensory experience. In other words, recognising that our sensory system generates experience is itself an experience. At no point can we be separated from experience. No matter what we do, we remain inseparable from it. We are the experience.

The idea of absolute reality, then, is also an experience. This may stem from our need for something permanent. Perhaps from ancient times, observing causation, humans noticed little change in nature compared to their own brief lives. They saw the sun rise and mountains stand upright for thousands of years, and from this the ideas of permanence and transience were born. Perhaps the need for permanence—for an absolute reality—is the outcome of this experience. Kant probably did not realise this. He believed that an absolute reality existed, though it lay beyond our comprehension.

There is no absolute or relative reality—only experience. Attempting to understand experience through symbols (themselves born from our experience and imagination) is a fundamental error.

In my view, knowledge (as an abstraction of experience) is a tool we must use with caution. It should serve to improve our well-being, where "our" means not only ourselves but also our neighbours and loved ones. Knowledge should be regarded as a model, but it must not be mistaken for the actual experience itself.
